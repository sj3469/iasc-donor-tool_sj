# Fundraising Best Practices: Quick Reference

---

## Donor Lifecycle and Segmentation

### Pipeline Stages

| Stage | Definition | Next Action |
|---|---|---|
| Prospect | Capacity + some affinity; no gift yet | Research, warm introduction |
| First-time donor | One gift | Immediate acknowledgment, cultivation |
| Repeat donor | 2+ gifts | Stewardship, upgrade asks |
| Major donor | Above org-defined threshold (e.g., $1,000+) | Personal relationship management |
| Lapsed donor | No gift in 18–36+ months | Re-engagement campaign |

### RFM Analysis

Scores donors on **Recency** (how recently?), **Frequency** (how often?), **Monetary value** (how much?).

For small nonprofits where most donors give once a year, frequency is a weak signal — weight recency and monetary value more heavily:

- **High priority:** recent gift + high amount (even if only once)
- **Re-engagement target:** high historical amount + not recent
- **Upgrade candidate:** consistent giver + modest amounts (growing relationship)
- **Low priority:** small, infrequent, not recent

RFM is a ranking tool, not a prediction. Use it to sort your list, not to make final decisions.

### Retention vs. Acquisition

Retaining a donor costs **5–10x less** than acquiring a new one. Confirm stewardship of existing donors is solid before investing in acquisition — a lapsed donor represents both lost future gifts and unrecovered acquisition cost.

**Sector benchmark:** ~40–45% annual retention for smaller nonprofits; 60%+ is strong; below 30% signals a stewardship problem.

### Moves Management

Sequence: **Identification → Qualification → Cultivation → Solicitation → Stewardship**

- **Identification:** Who belongs in the pipeline? (subscribers, event attendees, board referrals)
- **Qualification:** Capacity, affinity, and propensity (see CAP framework)
- **Cultivation:** Build the relationship before asking — events, personal updates, notes
- **Solicitation:** The ask; should feel natural if cultivation was done well
- **Stewardship:** Thank and report back; this is cultivation for the next gift

Jumping from identification directly to solicitation (skipping cultivation) sharply reduces response rates.

## Lapsed Donor Re-Engagement

- **Re-activation benchmark:** 5–15% of lapsed donors respond to a well-run campaign; rates vary by lapse length and relationship quality
- **Optimal window:** 18–24 months after last gift. Before 18 months, the donor may not feel lapsed; after 36 months, relationship cost rises significantly
- **Ask amount:** Start at ~50% of last gift to lower the barrier and reestablish the giving habit; upgrade in subsequent cycles

**Behavioral signals that suggest readiness:** renewed or active subscription, event attendance, recent email opens or clicks on content-driven sends, life change (new job, retirement)

**Win-back sequence (2–3 touches over 4–6 months):**
1. Touch 1 (month 1): Cultivation — share impact, no ask; personal if possible
2. Touch 2 (month 2–3): Soft ask, acknowledge the gap ("We've missed you"), graduated amount
3. Touch 3 (month 5–6): More direct appeal; if no response, move to lower-frequency maintenance list

More than three touches without response has diminishing returns.

**Channel priority:** Personal phone call > personalized email > direct mail. Mass email alone produces low re-activation rates for major donors.

## Major Gift Fundraising

### Donor Pyramid

~80% of dollars typically come from the top ~20% of donors. Even a small number of major gift relationships can represent a disproportionate share of revenue and deserve proportionally more cultivation time.

### CAP Qualification Framework

Before investing significant cultivation time, assess three dimensions:

- **Capacity:** Can they give at a meaningful level? (wealth indicators, philanthropic history)
- **Affinity:** Do they care about your mission? (subscriber, event attendee, intellectual alignment)
- **Propensity:** Have they shown a pattern of charitable giving? (gifts to peer orgs, board service)

High capacity without affinity is the most common false positive — wealth alone predicts little without mission connection.

### Personal Meetings Are Essential

Major gifts rarely happen without face-to-face cultivation. Written communication at the cultivation stage should aim to **secure a meeting**, not to ask for money.

**Planning estimate:** 4–6 qualified prospect meetings per major gift closed.

### Trip Planning: Center-Out Approach

1. **Start with strongest relationships:** Long-term major donors, board connections, anyone who gave in the past 12 months. These meetings are stewardship — they also generate referrals and warm introductions.
2. **Expand to warm prospects:** Engaged subscribers, lapsed donors showing re-engagement signals, wealth-screened prospects with known affinity.
3. **Avoid cold prospects** without a warm introduction in place.

Prioritize by: giving history + engagement signals + relationship depth + capacity. All four factors matter; capacity alone is not sufficient.

## Prospect Research and Wealth Screening

**Indicators:** real estate (most reliable, public record), stock/business ownership (SEC filings for public companies), prior philanthropic gifts (public disclosures), board service (signals philanthropic orientation).

**Key limitations:**
- Capacity ≠ willingness — the most important limitation
- Common false positives: wealth committed elsewhere, no giving history, illiquid assets
- Coverage gaps: 30–50% no-match rates, especially for younger donors or private wealth

Treat a high wealth score as a prompt to research further, not a solicitation trigger.

## Email and Digital Engagement

| Metric | Typical range | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Open rate | 15–25% | Apple Mail Privacy Protection has inflated reported opens since 2021; use clicks as primary signal |
| Click rate | 2–5% | Active interest; stronger signal than opens |
| Unsubscribe rate | 0.2–0.5% | Above 1% signals content or frequency problems |

**Engagement → giving:** Engaged subscribers are ~3–5x more likely to donate than disengaged ones. Email engagement is one of the best signals of giving readiness for non-donors.

A contact with no opens in 24 months is effectively unreachable by email; segment out to protect sender reputation.

**Segment messaging:**
- Prospects: mission-first, soft ask or no ask
- Lapsed donors: acknowledge prior relationship, graduated ask
- Active donors: stewardship and impact reporting; upgrade ask only when relationship supports it

## Key Metrics

| Metric | Benchmark |
|---|---|
| Donor retention rate | 40–45% avg; 60%+ strong |
| Re-activation rate (lapsed donors) | 5–15% |
| Average first gift (small nonprofits) | $50–150 |
| Average gift growth for retained donors | Major donors often give 10–100x their first gift over time |
| Cost per dollar raised — major gifts | $0.05–0.20 |
| Cost per dollar raised — direct mail | $0.20–0.50 |
| Cost per dollar raised — events | $0.50+ |

Use these benchmarks to set expectations and evaluate program health, not as rigid targets. Specialized audiences often diverge from sector averages.
